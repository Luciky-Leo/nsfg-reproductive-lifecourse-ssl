Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit the manuscript entitled "Reproductive life-course phenotyping in the National Survey of Family Growth: a self-supervised temporal survey study" for consideration in Reproductive Health.

This study uses public-use National Survey of Family Growth female respondent and pregnancy files from 2011-2013 through 2022-2023 to develop a leakage-controlled masked tabular self-supervised learning workflow for reproductive life-course phenotyping. The analysis integrates respondent-level domains including demographics, partnership history, contraception, pregnancy-history summaries, fertility-service use, impaired fecundity, insurance, poverty, and skip-pattern missingness. Earlier cycles were used for representation learning, 2017-2019 for phenotype selection, and 2022-2023 for within-NSFG temporal validation.

The main contribution is a reproducible population-survey phenotyping workflow rather than a clinical diagnostic or individual treatment-decision model. We explicitly interpret the results as survey-level phenotype discovery and endpoint enrichment. The validation endpoints are public-use NSFG survey endpoints or proxies, not hospital-verified clinical outcomes, and the temporal validation is within the NSFG public-use survey series rather than validation in a separate health-system dataset or registry. These boundaries are stated in the Abstract, Methods, Discussion, Conclusions, and availability statements.

We believe the manuscript fits Reproductive Health because it addresses population-level reproductive-health heterogeneity across contraception, pregnancy history, fertility care, fecundity limitation, partnership context, insurance, and socioeconomic domains using a reproducible public-use survey framework. The study is intended to complement single-domain NSFG surveillance reports by identifying interpretable reproductive life-course phenotypes and their descriptive enrichment for reproductive-health endpoints.

The manuscript includes source-data tables, figure assets, model metadata, and scripts in the submitted reproducibility package. Raw individual-level NSFG public-use records are not redistributed; the scripts point to CDC/NCHS public-use data portals and document the processing workflow. The submitted code package includes MIT license and citation metadata files.

All authors have approved the submitted version. The authors declare no competing interests, and no external funding was received for this study.
